Sick • injured • orphaned wildlife

Help safely. Act promptly.

This guide is for non-dangerous wildlife. Do not approach or handle an animal that could injure you.

Text 480-220-6930

Send a clear picture and your nearest major crossroads.

Before you intervene

Observe first.

Not every young animal found alone is orphaned. A parent may be nearby gathering food or waiting for people to leave.

  • Bleeding or visibly injured
  • Cold, weak, lethargic, or unresponsive
  • Unable to stand, walk, fly, or move normally
  • Covered with flies, fly eggs, or maggots
  • In immediate danger and unable to move to safety
  • A parent is known to be dead

If the animal appears healthy and is in a safe location: observe from a distance, keep children and pets away, and give the parent an opportunity to return. If one of the warning signs above applies, continue with the four steps below.

If the animal needs help

Follow these four steps.

1

Safely contain the animal

Use a ventilated cardboard box or secure pet carrier lined with paper towels or a smooth towel. Keep it closed in a dark, quiet area away from children and pets, and handle the animal as little as possible.

2

Keep the animal warm

Place a heating pad set on LOW under half of the box or carrier, or use a warm-water bottle wrapped in a towel. Leave part of the container unheated so the animal can move away.

Keep young waterfowl warm and dry. Do not place them in water.

Gentle warmth, never direct sun or high heat.
3

Do NOT give food or water

Do not feed the animal or give it water unless a wildlife rehabilitator tells you to. Never pour, drip, or force liquid into its mouth or beak.

Warmth, quiet, and prompt care come first.
4

Contact Crystal promptly

Text 480-220-6930 with a clear picture, your nearest major crossroads, and what you observed.

Be prepared to transport the animal unless other arrangements are made.
